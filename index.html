Swipe Up

To Begin

The Linotype operator uses a keyboard to assemble individual brass molds of characters (known as matrices, or mats) into a line of type. Each assembled line is automatically cast into a single lead bar, called
a slug. Unlike hand typesetting, an entire line can be cast in only a few seconds. The mats automatically return to a holding place (known as the magazine) to be reused later. The slugs are removed from the machine to be placed in galley trays where the page is composed.

A Miraculous Machine

An Intricate System

 

Listen to the Linotype

The Linotype keyboard has 90 keys: lower case letters, upper case letters, & symbols. To speed operation, letters are arranged based on frequency of use. As the keyboard operator types, the corresponding mats are released from the magazine in the proper sequence to form lines of sentences. Spaces between words are inserted by space bands that automatically adjust to create justified lines. There is no backspace or deletion key, so the operator cannot delete typing errors. Instead, he or she runs a finger down the first two rows of keys so that the line reads “etaoin shrdlu.” This signifies that the slug needs to be discarded. However, sometimes “etaoin shrdlu” would wind up in print and eventually it became a joke in the printing industry.

Casting is triggered by the keyboard operator at the completion of
every line. Once a line of brass mats enters the casting mechanism,
it is pressed against a mold where hot lead is injected, which forms a slug of metal type. Each slug is then ejected into a tray in the proper order to form columns of type, and the line of brass mats is returned

to the magazine. The full casting cycle takes less than nine seconds. The keyboard and distributor sections operate through cams which

are synchronized with the casting mechanism. The casting of the line occurs simultaneously with the return of the mats used for the prior

line and the composition of the next line, greatly enhancing the productivity of the machine.

The Linotype’s most significant innovation was to automate the distribution step. After casting is completed, the lines of mats are pushed to an elevator arm that raises them to the distributor at the top of the magazine. Like an ordinary house key, each mat has a unique pattern of teeth at the top, by which it hangs from the distributor bar. Each channel of the magazine has a corresponding pattern of teeth. As the mats move along the distributor bar, they drop into the appropriate channel, enabling each mat to return to its proper place for reuse.
